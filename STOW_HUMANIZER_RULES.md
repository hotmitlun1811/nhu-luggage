# HUMANIZER RULES — STOW DA NANG
*Apply these rules to every caption, post, email, or WhatsApp message before publishing.*

---

## THE CORE TEST

Imagine you're behind the Stow counter. A traveler just walked in, bag on their back, a little sweaty, flight in 8 hours. Read the draft out loud to them.

If it sounds like a hotel brochure, a travel startup's Instagram, or a ChatGPT marketing output — rewrite it. If you'd feel awkward saying it to an actual person standing in front of you — rewrite it.

Stow speaks like a helpful local. Not a brand trying to sound like one.

---

## 1. FORBIDDEN CHARACTERS & PUNCTUATION

### Em dashes (—) — never use
The single strongest AI writing signal. Appears constantly in generated travel copy.

- ✗ "Drop your bags — and explore the city with total freedom."
- ✓ "Drop your bags. Explore the city."
- ✓ "Drop your bags and explore the city."

**Fix:** Period and a new sentence, or a comma, or cut it entirely.

### Ellipsis (…) — never use
AI uses it for dramatic trail-off. Stow doesn't trail off.

- ✗ "Your bags are in good hands… go enjoy the city."
- ✓ "Your bags are in good hands. Go enjoy the city."

### Decorative bullet points in running copy
Fine in pricing tables and step-by-step guides. Wrong in captions, posts, or conversational copy.

### Excessive bold
One emphasis per message at most. If everything is highlighted, nothing is.

### Safe • Simple • Supportive
This is the visual tagline in headers only. Never write it as running prose in captions or posts.

---

## 2. FORBIDDEN SENTENCE STRUCTURES

### The "Imagine" opener
The most common AI travel marketing structure. Never.

- ✗ "Imagine arriving in Da Nang, bags checked, ready to explore..."
- ✓ "Flight lands at 8am. Hotel room isn't ready until 2pm. We're open at 7."

### Abstract benefit statements
AI names the feeling instead of the fact.

- ✗ "Experience the freedom of luggage-free exploration."
- ✓ "Your bag weighs 14kg. Leave it with us. Come back at 10pm."

### The transformation narrative
Stow stores bags. It doesn't transform people. Don't promise transformation.

- ✗ "Transform your Da Nang experience with seamless, worry-free storage."
- ✓ "Check out at noon. Bus leaves at 9pm. We hold your stuff in between."

### The problem-agitate-solution formula written badly
Fine as a structure. Wrong when the language is polished instead of honest.

- ✗ "Are you tired of lugging heavy bags through Da Nang's heat? Frustrated by not having a place to store your belongings? There's a better way."
- ✓ "Most people spend their last day in Da Nang stuck near their hotel because they have nowhere to leave their bags. We fix that."

### The noun list with periods
AI lists things by putting a period after each one for dramatic effect.

- ✗ "Safety. Simplicity. Trust."
- ✗ "Photo receipt. ID tag. CCTV. Locked storage."
- ✓ "Photo receipt when you drop off. Locked storage while you're out. Same bag back when you return."

### The setup-colon-reveal
AI creates fake tension with X: [dramatic reveal].

- ✗ "The result: total peace of mind."
- ✗ "Our promise: your belongings, always safe."
- ✓ "Your stuff stays safe. That's the whole thing."

### Parallel triplets used as emphasis
- ✗ "Safe. Simple. Supportive." (as body copy — this is a header-only tagline)
- ✗ "We keep it safe. We keep it simple. We keep it supportive."
- ✓ Let the three values live in the header. Don't repeat them as a chorus throughout.

## 6. WORD-LEVEL AI SIGNALS

**Travel marketing buzzwords — avoid or cut:**
seamless · hassle-free · worry-free · stress-free · carefree · effortless · convenient (just state what's convenient) · affordable (just state the price) · trusted (just show why) · reliable (just show how) · vibrant · bustling · dynamic · nestled · situated · premier · world-class · curated · thoughtfully designed · unlock · unleash · empower · passionate about · dedicated to · committed to · innovative · solutions (as a noun for anything)

**Phrases that read as AI-generated in this context:**
- "peace of mind" — used once max (it's in the brand positioning). Not in every post.
- "in the heart of Da Nang" — tourist map language
- "your Da Nang experience" — too abstract
- "travel smarter, not harder" — travel-brand cliché
- "your home away from home" — generic expat copy
- "hub for travelers" — startup language
- "your go-to storage solution" — generic service-brand language
- "at the intersection of convenience and security" — AI abstraction
- "we take your trust seriously" — AI trust-claiming (show don't tell)
- "rest assured" — AI reassurance phrase

## 7. STRUCTURAL AI PATTERNS

### The "5 Reasons Why" article format
AI defaults to numbered reasons and listicles. Stow's content should read as observations, not content marketing.

### The FAQ that nobody asked
AI generates FAQs for questions no real customer has phrased that way. Use actual WhatsApp questions from real customers as the source.

### Every post ends with a CTA
Not every post needs to close with "Book now" or a link. Some posts exist to be useful. Let them be useful.

### The feature list disguised as storytelling
AI strings features into a story structure but the features are still just a list. Real storytelling starts from a person's situation.

### Consistent rhythm across all posts
AI writes all posts with the same cadence. Stow should mix long explanatory sentences with single punchy ones. Then a very short one.

## 8. THE FIVE REWRITE TESTS

**Test 1 — The Counter Test**
Would you say this to a traveler who just walked in? If it would sound weird coming from a human behind a counter, rewrite it.

**Test 2 — The Specificity Test**
Replace every abstract word with the specific fact behind it. "Convenient location" → "5 minutes from Han Market." "Affordable pricing" → "60,000₫ for the day." If you can't make it specific, cut it.

**Test 3 — The Em Dash Scan**
Search for —. Replace every instance. No exceptions.

**Test 4 — The Transformation Scan**
Search for: imagine, experience (as verb), journey (metaphorical), freedom, transform, seamless, worry-free. Replace every instance with the actual thing.

**Test 5 — The Opener/Closer Check**
Is the first sentence on the forbidden opener list? Is the last sentence on the forbidden closer list? Rewrite both if yes.

## 9. POSITIVE PATTERNS — STOW'S REAL VOICE

These come from the brand story, the brand positioning, and the Supportive principle. Use these as the positive reference.

---

### Pattern 1 — Start from their situation, not your service

The Stow brand story opens: *"Da Nang is home to thousands of foreigners who live, work remotely, and stay long-term — not just tourists passing through. Every few months, their visa expires and they must leave for a visa run, days to weeks away."*

It starts with the person's actual life. Not with Stow.

Real examples:
> "Your flight lands at 8am. Hotel check-in is at 2pm. That's 6 hours with nowhere to put your bags."

> "Visa run this week. You've got a laptop, two bags, and a bike you can't take with you."

Wrong:
> "Stow offers flexible luggage storage solutions for travelers and expats in Da Nang."

---

### Pattern 2 — Specific times, prices, places — not moods

Stow speaks in facts. Never in feelings.

- ✓ "Open 7am to 10pm. Every day."
- ✓ "60,000₫ for the whole day. Oversized bag? Add 30,000₫."
- ✓ "5 minutes from Han Market. 10 minutes from My Khe Beach."
- ✗ "Conveniently located in central Da Nang."
- ✗ "Affordable storage for every budget."
- ✗ "Always here when you need us."

---

### Pattern 3 — "No one wants to..." (honest empathy, from brand story)

The brand story uses this construction: *"No one wants to pay for an empty room just to hold a spot, not everyone has a friend they trust enough to leave a laptop, a bike, daily belongings with for weeks."*

It names the real uncomfortable thing. No softening, no pivot to "but we have great news."

Real examples:
> "No one wants to spend their last morning in Da Nang killing time in a café with a 15kg bag at their feet."

> "Not everyone moving apartments has a friend with a spare room and the patience to store three boxes for two weeks."

---

### Pattern 4 — Short imperative sequence

Three actions. One per line. No connectives.

> "Drop your bags. Come back at 10pm. Done."

> "Show your tag. Take your bag. Go catch your flight."

> "Message us first. Walk in. Under 3 minutes."

This is the closest Stow gets to a slogan structure in daily copy. Use it sparingly — once or twice a month.

---

### Pattern 5 — WhatsApp directness

Stow's primary contact is WhatsApp. The copy should feel like what a helpful local actually sends.

- ✓ "We're open. Drop by anytime before 10pm. Just message first so we're ready."
- ✓ "Flight delayed? No problem. Just let us know your new pickup time."
- ✓ "Need your bag delivered to the hotel? Just ask — we can arrange it."
- ✗ "Our dedicated team is available to assist with any schedule changes."
- ✗ "Please contact us to arrange alternative pickup arrangements."

The Supportive principle says it best: *"Need someone to bring your bag to you? Just ask."* That's the tone. Short. Warm. No sales pressure.

---

### Pattern 6 — Da Nang specificity

Reference real places, real rhythms, real Da Nang situations. Not generic "Vietnam travel" imagery.

- ✓ "Overnight train to Hoi An? We'll hold your bag until you're back."
- ✓ "Marble Mountains, Ba Na Hills, My Son — all easier without a 15kg bag."
- ✓ "Visa run to Bangkok. Back in 4 days. Your stuff will be here."
- ✗ "Explore the wonders of Vietnam's Central Coast."
- ✗ "Da Nang's beautiful beaches await — luggage-free."

---

### Pattern 7 — Rhythm: long, then short

Stow's brand story uses long explanatory sentences followed by short, landing sentences.

> "Your belongings don't need to follow that rhythm with you. We keep them safe, in the right place, at the right time — so when you return to Da Nang, everything is still there, ready."

In daily copy, aim for this rhythm:
> "Most people spend their last day in Da Nang stuck near their hotel because they've checked out but can't board until 11pm. We fix that."

Not every sentence the same length. Let the short sentence land.

---

### The Test Question

Before publishing, ask:

*Would this fit inside the Stow brand story paragraph as a natural sentence?*

If it fits alongside *"Your belongings don't need to follow that rhythm with you"* — the voice is right.

If it reads like a travel Instagram account or a hotel welcome card — rewrite it.
